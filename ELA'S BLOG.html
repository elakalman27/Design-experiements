Topic: Colour + typography

This week’s lecture discussed colour and typography. Both these elements are familiar to me as a graphic designer, however, it was interesting relearning everything in the context of web design.

Colour: colour is an area I am still learning to master but not quite there. The classic colour wheel theory is always helpful to go out but discovering the tool of adobe colour was a great help. It’s beginning to become an integral part of my design process.

Typography: Again, typography is a familiar area of graphic design that I now see from a different perspective in the context of web design. Understanding sizes in terms of H1, H2, H3 and still does not come to me naturally. I hope that through the process of creating the website and using software such as Figma and Webflow, I will begin to think of scaling in those terms. Focusing on seniors as my target audience, I checked what are appropriate sizes for body text, ensuring I wouldn’t make them too small. According to accessibleweb.com, body text should be about 16px and never smaller than 12px.

The workshop this week was incredibly valuable, teaching us web design literacy and training the eye to spot errors.

Due to my extension on the project, I am still working on Assignment 1 at the moment. I have a very clear understanding of the project and where it is supposed to be. Week 7

Topic: Composition + affordances

This week’s lecture covered composition and affordances. The website of the week for this lecture stood out to me. Having been collecting more knowledge of web design now I find things more impressive than I did before, being able to appreciate the work and creativity that goes into projects. I find that accessibility in web design is in the middle of bad design and design that is so cool and innovative that it becomes inaccessible again. This often means that an accessible site will be boring. However, this website proves that it is possible to make a site that is both unique and exciting while very clear on its intentions and accessible to all audiences.

The composition portion of the lecture was like finding the answer to a difficult riddle. I am still getting the hang of composition, which translates slightly differently between art, graphic design and web design. Grids have been a useful tool to me but in the past, I’ve struggled to understand what it means to follow a grid. Understanding the difference between columns, gutters and margins and seeing visual examples of different layout types was fantastic. Consistency is sometimes difficult for me when designing only due to a lack of knowledge of shortcuts. In project 2.1 I discovered some typography features in Figma that make consistency so easy to create, making the prototype instantly cleaner. My understanding of scale, negative space and balance is growing through the creation of the prototype. While I feel that my eye is trained to recognise that an existing composition looks good, I still find it hard to create them. A composition feature that I like and am integrating into my design is flow, using colours and background elements to make the experience of the website feel more fluid and lightly guiding them through the site.

Understanding affordances seems to be integral to web design. So much of the communication to users in web design is visual, the show not tell principle. As a user it seems that affordances like buttons, links, input fields etc should be obvious since they are already so integrated into web design and our everyday use of them. However, as a designer, we have to think of it in terms of accessibility and clear communication, which adds a layer of intention to each button, link and CTA. This is very relevant to my website as I am designing for an older audience that may not have as much experience and familiarity with the web. I need to find visual solutions that will be appropriate, clear and easy to use but also not mock the intelligence of the user. Understanding visual hierarchy will be of great assistance to me here as it is one of the easiest ways to guide a user towards an action.

Project 2.2 reflection:

Week 12

Topic: Assignment 3 reflection + final thoughts

Going into assignment 3, my main focus was the coding part of it. I’ve kept my journal entries quite consistent throughout the trimester. Coding was a big scary part of this course that I tried to avoid for as long as possible, which almost cost me. However, I’m very happy with how this assignment went.

I watched all the coding tutorials again to prepare for the assignment. After my experience with CMS, I had an understanding of how flexboxes and grids work and the general rules and defaults of websites. I quickly gained a good understanding of the coding basics. HTML is much easier than I expected. Coding the tutorial tasks was so much fun, it was exciting to see the instant results of writing lines of code appearing on the screen.

For my journal, I wanted a split screen layout where one side scrolls and the other doesn’t. It took a few tries and a few tutorials, but eventually, I made it. A regret that I have is designing the layout in Flexbox instead of a grid. Instead of following the advice in the grids tutorials of starting with a mobile layout and using media queries to adjust the screen to larger sizes, I made the classing mistake of starting with a desktop size. I thought that if I lay it out in a flex-direction row, I could use a media query to simply adjust it to the column when it hits a certain size, with the sticky left side at the top and the cards down the bottom. Unfortunately, I couldn’t figure it out here. The blog is quite responsive though, everything being readable and aesthetic until smaller than tablet size. Another original plan was for cards on the main page to be expandable with a “read more’ button. I learned how to do this for one card, and it worked beautifully, however when I duplicated the card the buttons still affected only the original card. My current knowledge of buttons was not good enough for me to adjust that on my own, however, I do plan to learn how to fix that in the future. Instead, the blog has 2 main pages with the entries on the right. Throughout the process, I managed to get a good hang of writing HTML and CSS, and the blog eventually looked exactly how I wanted it to.

I enjoyed this project, I did a lot better than I originally thought I would be able to. I found coding somewhat easier than CMS because coding grants you complete control over the website. If I don’t know how to control something then I search it up and learn to do it myself instead of trying to learn the rules of a different website. The blog is by no means perfect, if I had more time I would prefect a lot more of its rough edges. However, I am still exptrememly happy with what I was able to make.

Something I realised is that my prior lack of knowledge of coding and CMS in project 2.1 caused a domino effect of struggle. If I had designed the layout of the website according to the visual as well as technical rules of web design, designing it in either CMS or coding would be much easier. Things like the background, rounded shapes that made no sense etc. set me up for confusion and struggle in 2.2. However, that learning curve was valuable and left me much more knowledgeable. Although going into the projects already knowing what I was doing could be nice. But after all, this course is intended for learning.

My knowledge and appreciation for web design and especially coding is much more vast now. I’m very excited to continue studying web design and to implement my acquired knowledge into future projects. In the future I’d like to look into web design as a means of storytelling, mastering the elements of site building to tell narratives. Or vice versa, implementing elements of storytelling in web design. My skills are still limited but have grown exponentially throughout this course and I’m excited to see how they will keep expanding.
